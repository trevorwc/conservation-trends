Science in a crisis

Science is key to combatting the biodiversity crisis

The field of conservation science has itself become increasingly diverse. In addition to the canonical conservationists collecting samples in remote landscapes, advances in cellular biology as well as computational power and techniques means that scientists have an increasing number of tools at their disposal.

We assessed yearly trends the topics of scientific papers through an analysis of their abstracts. After accessing 81,958 journal abstracts related to the keyword "conservation" since 2013, we filtered to those published in 85 "disciplinary journals," which we defined as those with more than 100 articles related to conservation, leaving us with 68,065 abstracts. The model classified the abstracts into 667 topics. Nearly half of the abstracts fit the "intercept" term that can be thought of as unclassified or too difficult to classify.

In order to ensure that we were only accessing the highest quality data, we only performed subsequent analysis on those topics that had more than 100 articles, then filtered to those with more than 50% probability of being in their assigned class. This left us with 13,661 abstracts remaining.

Some viewers might notice that there are actually 11 topics plotted- this is because the gut microbiome and sperm are both tied for 10th place in terms of total count. Some notable trends stick out, including the increase in reserach about fires, agricultural yields, and pollinators. Some of the topics in this list of the most popular are likely familiar to those that follow the zietgeist of popular science, while others reflect long-running disciplinary interests.

The previous charts give a good understanding of trends in science in absolute terms, but which topics have changed the most in relative terms? This metric can give a better idea of which topics have become relatively more or less popular, versus those that have long been mainstream.

The relative change in each topic's popularity compared to its average from 2013-2015 is plotted below in an interactive bar chart that allows users to compare as many of few topics as are of interest.

We see that the five topics that have seemingly fallen out of favor are related to working conditions, demography, queens/workers, health care, and the genetics of salmon and trout.

Interstingly, these "out of favor" topics may point to a limitation of the model's ability to understand the meaning of words given context, or could represent overly narrow categories. For example, the meaning of queens, workers, and colony most likely relate to pollinators, but these articles were not included in the pollinators category. It is plausible that this research represents a distinct subject, but without additional manual analysis, the model is unable to differntiate these possibilities.

Innovation in science

In addition to identifying the key topics and their changes over time, a key question is whether there has been any trend in innovation in conservation science. There are many ways that innovation can be measured, but we were specifically interested in how natural language processing can be used to measure innovation from the abstract. This was partially due to the fact that other methods, such as analyzing trends in citations, would not be possible as this is not included in this dataset.

In additon to the "innovation" sentiment analysis, we explored semantic complexity as a measure of scientific complexity. This is undoubtedly an imperfect measure- as one mark of a good scientist is finding simplicity in complexity- but is based on the reasoning that more complex language will be necessary to write about more complex topics.

These two metrics provide seemingly contradictory results. In order to test the hypothesis that more innovative scientists may simply be using simpler language, we tested regressed innovation against grade. The two were significantly positively related, indicating that more innovative studies tended to use more complex language.

Because our two different methods found different results regarding the change in innovation, we are unable to come to difinitive conclusions at this time. It is possible that approaches outside of NLP, such as citation analysis, might be necessary.
